NFPA 70E and Arc Flash Safety

By Steve Stephenson   Published 09/05/2013

Arc flash, a rapid, explosive discharge of electrical energy, poses real dangers to those working around energized electrical equipment. To combat these dangers, NFPA 70E, entitled Electrical Safety in the Workplace, was written to help create an electrically safe working environment as the first line of defense against arc flash and other electrical hazards.

The most effective way of ensuring worker safety around electrical equipment is to make sure this equipment is de-energized, so NFPA 70E recommends a series of steps to guide you in isolating equipment from all possible power sources, tagging systems appropriately, and verifying that equipment is safe to work on. The clear intent of these steps is to create the safest possible work environment by de-energizing and security the equipment you’ll be working on.

NFPA 70E and OSHA

Sometimes de-energizing equipment isn’t practical, or even possible. Ventilators, life support or other critical equipment typically won’t be de-energized for obvious reasons, and some continuously-operating equipment can’t be de-energized by design. Even the very nature of the work you need to do can mean equipment must remain energized.

NFPA 70E lays out six steps that employers should take to reduce the risk to workers and stay in compliance with OSHA regulations when work must be done on energized equipment:

  • Create a facility safety program with defined responsibilities
  • Calculate arc flash hazards for relevant equipment
  • Provide appropriate PPE for live work
  • Train workers on arc flash hazards and safe work practices
  • Provide appropriate tools for working with energized equipment
  • Place warning labels on equipment that poses an arc flash risk

Arc Flash Warning Labels for NFPA 70E

NFPA 70E section 130.5(C) requires "Electrical equipment such as switchboards, panelboards, industrial control panels, meter socket enclosures, and motor control centers that are in other than dwelling units and are likely to require examination, adjustment, servicing or maintenance while energized…” to be marked with a label that includes specific information about information from system voltage to PPE requirements to the arc flash boundary.
